# Quantum Computing

Quantum computing is a type of computation whose operations can harness the phenomena of quantum mechanics, such as superposition, interference, and entanglement. Devices that perform quantum computations are known as quantum computers.

## Fundamental Concepts

### Qubits
The fundamental unit of quantum information is the quantum bit, or qubit. Unlike a classical bit that can be either 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. This property allows quantum computers to process a vast number of possibilities concurrently.

### Superposition
Superposition refers to the quantum mechanical principle that a physical system exists in all its possible states simultaneously, until it is measured. When measured, the system collapses into one of its possible states. This is what gives quantum computing its potential for massive parallelism.

### Entanglement
Quantum entanglement is a physical phenomenon that occurs when a group of particles are generated, interact, or share spatial proximity in a way such that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently. Instead, the quantum state must be described for the system as a whole.

## Applications

### Cryptography
Quantum computers pose a significant threat to current public-key cryptography systems. Shor's algorithm can efficiently factor large integers, which would break RSA encryption. This has led to the development of post-quantum cryptography.

### Optimization
Many real-world optimization problems are computationally intractable for classical computers. Quantum annealing and variational quantum algorithms offer potential speedups for problems in logistics, finance, and machine learning.

### Drug Discovery
Simulating molecular interactions is extremely resource-intensive on classical computers. Quantum computers could model these interactions natively, potentially accelerating drug discovery and materials science.

## Current Challenges

Quantum decoherence — the loss of quantum information to the environment — remains the primary obstacle. Modern quantum computers require extreme cooling (near absolute zero) and elaborate error correction schemes to maintain coherence long enough to perform useful computations.
