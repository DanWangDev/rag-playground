# Climate Change

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. These shifts may be natural, but since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to the burning of fossil fuels which produces heat-trapping gases.

## Causes

### Greenhouse Gas Emissions
The primary cause of contemporary climate change is the emission of greenhouse gases (GHGs), particularly carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4). These gases trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere through the greenhouse effect. Since the Industrial Revolution, atmospheric CO2 concentrations have increased from approximately 280 parts per million to over 420 parts per million.

### Deforestation
Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere. Widespread deforestation, particularly in tropical regions like the Amazon rainforest, reduces this capacity and releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere.

## Effects

### Rising Global Temperatures
The global average temperature has increased by approximately 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era. The past decade (2014-2024) was the warmest on record. This warming is not uniform — polar regions are warming at roughly twice the global average rate.

### Sea Level Rise
Sea levels have risen by about 20 centimeters since 1880, with the rate of rise nearly doubling in the past two decades. This is driven by thermal expansion of seawater and melting of land-based ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica.

### Extreme Weather Events
Climate change increases the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events, including heatwaves, droughts, floods, and tropical cyclones. Attribution science now allows researchers to quantify how much climate change contributed to specific extreme events.

## Mitigation Strategies

### Renewable Energy
Transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources — solar, wind, hydroelectric, and geothermal — is central to climate mitigation. The cost of solar photovoltaic modules has fallen by over 90% since 2010.

### Carbon Capture
Direct air capture and carbon capture and storage technologies aim to remove CO2 from the atmosphere or prevent its release from industrial sources. While promising, these technologies remain expensive and energy-intensive at scale.

### Policy Frameworks
The Paris Agreement (2015) established a framework for global climate action, with countries committing to nationally determined contributions (NDCs) to reduce emissions. The goal is to limit warming to well below 2°C, preferably to 1.5°C.
